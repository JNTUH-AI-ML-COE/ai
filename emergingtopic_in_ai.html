Emerging Topics in Modern AI

2025 Graduate Syllabus

Course Overview

This two-semester graduate-level sequence explores cutting-edge research and emerging architectures in modern AI as of 2025. Students will gain a broad, horizontal perspective on advances across multiple subfields (natural language processing, computer vision, multimodal learning, robotics, etc.), while engaging deeply with research papers and open-ended projects. The course emphasizes latest developments ‚Äì from large-scale foundation models and generative AI systems to autonomous agentic AI and early steps toward AGI. We will balance theoretical concepts with hands-on experimentation, critical discussions of research readings, and a focus on ASL-3/ASL-4 level AI safety considerations (the frontier AI Safety Levels defined for advanced AI deployments). By the end, students should understand the state of the art in multimodal generative models (e.g. text, image, audio, video generation), advanced model self-improvement techniques, "AI Scientist 2.0"-style autonomous research agents, and emerging paradigms for self-adapting and self-directed AI systems on the path toward AGI.

üìÖ Format

The course meets twice weekly. Semester 1 builds core knowledge of modern AI advances, while Semester 2 delves into frontier research and open problems. Each week includes research paper readings (drawn from recent conferences and university courses) and discussion. Evaluation is through reading responses, a midterm exam (Semester 1), and a term project (Semester 2) where students explore an emerging AI topic in depth.

üìö Prerequisites

Strong background in machine learning and deep learning. Prior exposure to NLP or computer vision is recommended. Experience with neural network frameworks and some familiarity with reinforcement learning will be helpful.

Semester 1: Foundations of Modern AI and Generative Models

Focus: Key breakthroughs that defined the current state-of-the-art ‚Äì large language models, multimodal foundation models, and generative architectures across different data modalities. We examine how these models are built and the new capabilities (and challenges) they bring.
  1. Foundation Models and the New AI Paradigm: Overview of foundation models (e.g. GPT-3/4, BERT, etc.) and how large-scale pretraining has led to a paradigm shift in AI. We discuss the defining traits of foundation models ‚Äì trained on broad data, adaptable to many tasks ‚Äì and review the Stanford "Opportunities & Risks of Foundation Models" report (Bommasani et al., 2021) for context. Topics include the homogenization of methodologies across fields and emergence of new capabilities through scale. Real-world examples (ChatGPT, Copilot) illustrate the transformational impact of large language models (LLMs).
    Reading: Bommasani et al. (2021); OpenAI GPT-3 paper.
  2. Transformer Architecture & Scaling Laws: Deep dive into the Transformer neural network architecture that underpins most modern AI models. We cover the self-attention mechanism and why Transformers enable efficient training on massive datasets (Vaswani et al., 2017). Discussion of scaling laws: as model parameters and data increase, performance often improves predictably ‚Äì until emergent behaviors appear. Students examine evidence of emergent abilities in large models (e.g. GPT-3's zero-shot learning) and debate recent studies questioning if such emergent traits are real or artifacts.
    Reading: "Attention Is All You Need" and Kaplan et al. on scaling laws.
  3. Pre-training, Fine-Tuning, and Alignment (RLHF): How foundation models are trained and adapted. We discuss self-supervised pre-training on web-scale data and techniques to fine-tune models for specific tasks. Instruction tuning and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) are introduced as key alignment methods to make AI outputs more helpful and safe. For example, OpenAI's InstructGPT used RLHF to align an LLM with user intent across many tasks. We also cover Constitutional AI and other strategies for aligning models without direct human labels.
    Reading: Ouyang et al. (2022) on InstructGPT; Anthropic's "Constitutional AI" paper.
  4. Vision and Multimodal Foundation Models: Extending foundation models beyond text. We study models that learn unified representations for images and text, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), which learns visual concepts by matching images with captions. We also survey multimodal transformers that handle vision+language inputs (e.g. Flamingo, OFA). Key concepts: contrastive image-text pre-training, visual question answering, and the transition from modality-specific models to general-purpose assistants that handle text, images, etc. (e.g. Microsoft's Kosmos-1). Multimodal foundation models mark a step from specialist models to unified AI assistants.
    Reading: Li et al. (2023) "Multimodal Foundation Models: From Specialists to General-Purpose Assistants".
  5. Generative Models for Images (VAEs, GANs, Diffusion): Overview of generative AI for visual data. We start with VAEs and GANs, then focus on diffusion models which powered the 2022‚Äì2023 text-to-image boom (e.g. DALL¬∑E 2, Stable Diffusion). We explain how diffusion models iteratively refine random noise into coherent images, and why their diversity and quality surpassed prior methods. Case studies: Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) as an open-source image generator. We also discuss ethical implications like deepfakes and generative art. Lab: Students prompt an image model to create visuals and analyze results.
    Reading: Ho et al. (2020) on Denoising Diffusion; Rombach et al. (2022) on Latent Diffusion.
  6. Generative Models for Audio and Music: Generative AI in the audio domain. We cover text-to-speech models (Tacotron, VALL-E) and recent breakthroughs in music generation. Example: MusicLM (Google, 2023), a model that generates high-fidelity music from text descriptions[1]. Students listen to samples from MusicLM ‚Äì e.g. "jazzy piano solo" ‚Äì to appreciate capabilities and current limitations (like controllability and coherence in long compositions). We also touch on voice cloning and speech synthesis advances.
    Reading: Dhariwal et al. (2020) "Jukebox: Music Generation"; Google Blog on MusicLM[1].
  7. Generative Models for Video and 3D Worlds: The frontier of generative AI ‚Äì creating dynamic video and simulated environments. We survey text-to-video models (e.g. Meta's Make-A-Video and Runway Gen-2) and discuss challenges in temporal consistency. A highlight is DeepMind's Genie world models, culminating in Genie 3 (2025) which generates interactive 3D environments from text in real-time. Genie 3 can produce a persistent, physics-consistent world at 24 fps, representing a step toward unlimited virtual training grounds for agents. We consider how world models are key for training embodied agents and are seen as "stepping stones on the path to AGI".
    Reading: DeepMind Technical Blog on Genie 3; Ho et al. (2022) on Video Diffusion (VDM).
  8. AI for Programming and Code Generation: How generative models impact software development. We study code-generating LLMs like OpenAI's Codex (2021) which powers GitHub Copilot, and newer open models (StarCoder, Code Llama). These models translate natural language to code and vice versa. We explore their architecture (often GPT-based with code fine-tuning) and evaluate capabilities: e.g. Codex can solve programming tasks or generate functions from docstrings. Topics include prompt techniques for code (test-driven prompts) and limitations (bugs, security issues).
    Reading: Chen et al. (2021) "Evaluating Large Language Models Trained on Code".
  9. AI in Science and Medicine (Case Studies): Examination of emerging AI applications in scientific research, showing the horizontal impact of modern AI. We discuss AlphaFold2 (DeepMind, 2021) as a breakthrough in biology ‚Äì using deep learning to predict 3D protein structures with atomic accuracy, effectively solving the 50-year protein folding problem[2]. We also highlight how foundation models are being adapted in medicine (e.g. biomedicine-specific LLMs like PubMedGPT, and generative models for drug discovery). For example, generative models can propose novel molecular structures (Medicinal chemistry via diffusion models). Students are encouraged to consider how techniques from earlier weeks (like diffusion or large LMs) can accelerate scientific discovery.
    Reading: Jumper et al. (2021) on AlphaFold; Zhavoronkov et al. (2022) on AI-designed drugs.
  10. Efficient Adaptation of Large Models: As models reach billions of parameters, researchers have developed techniques to fine-tune and deploy them more efficiently. We cover parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods such as LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation), which updates only small low-rank weight matrices, and adapter modules. These approaches allow adapting giant models to new tasks with few trainable parameters. We also discuss model compression (distillation, quantization) and how 8-bit/4-bit quantization can drastically reduce serving costs. This week includes a hands-on exercise applying a LoRA fine-tune to a pre-trained model.
    Reading: Hu et al. (2021) on LoRA; Ganesh et al. (2023) on quantization.
  11. Challenges: Hallucinations, Bias, and Evaluation: Despite their prowess, modern AI models have well-known issues. We examine the hallucination problem in LLMs ‚Äì models sometimes fabricate plausible-sounding but incorrect facts. Students analyze examples of GPT-4 hallucinations and discuss why this occurs (next-token prediction limitations, lack of grounding). We also address biases that models learn from data (gender, racial biases, etc.) and discuss techniques for auditing and mitigating bias. The need for better evaluation metrics is highlighted: traditional accuracy metrics often fail to capture these failure modes, spurring new benchmarks (e.g. truthfulness, toxicity, and the BIG-bench for general tasks).
    Reading: Ji et al. (2023) "Survey on Hallucination in NLG"; NIST report on AI Bias.
  12. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): One solution to hallucination and knowledge limitations is augmenting models with external data. We cover RAG architectures where an LLM is coupled with a vector database or search API to retrieve relevant information. For instance, open-domain QA systems (like Bing's GPT-4-based chat) use web search results to ground responses. We discuss implementations such as Retro, REALM, and Toolformer, which allow models to call external tools or APIs. Students will build a simple RAG pipeline: using embeddings to fetch documents that help a smaller LM answer questions correctly.
    Reading: Lewis et al. (2020) "Retrieval-Augmented Generation"; Schick et al. (2023) Toolformer (LLM that decides when to use tools).
  13. Integration and Project Proposal Discussions: In this capstone week for Semester 1, we reflect on how the pieces fit together. How might one integrate language, vision, and other modalities into a single AI agent? We revisit the goal of general-purpose AI assistants and identify open issues from Semester 1's topics (e.g. model trustworthiness, data efficiency). Students present proposals for their Semester 2 research projects, framing them in context of course topics. No new readings. (Midterm exam to be scheduled around this week covering Weeks 1‚Äì12 content.)

Semester 2: Advanced Topics and Frontier Research (AGI and Beyond)

Focus: Cutting-edge and exploratory topics that push toward higher levels of AI autonomy, reasoning, and "AGI-oriented" capabilities. We explore how to make AI systems more agentic, adaptive, and self-directed, along with the safety and ethical challenges of such progress. This part is research-heavy ‚Äì we'll dissect recent papers and even unfinished ideas in the quest for more general AI.
  1. Beyond Pattern Matching ‚Äì LLM Reasoning and Planning: Modern LLMs excel at pattern recognition but lack true reasoning and long-horizon planning. This week examines methods to impart deliberate reasoning to language models. We discuss prompting techniques like Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting, which has models explicitly reason through problems step-by-step, and the ReAct framework (combining reasoning and acting). We also explore enabling planning: e.g. using LLMs to generate high-level plans or break tasks into sub-tasks. A key question: Can an LLM move from reactive Q&A to "conscious," validated reasoning needed for AGI?
    Reading: Wei et al. (2022) on Chain-of-Thought; Zhou et al. (2022) on ReAct.
  2. Tool Use and External Interfaces: We study how AI agents can extend their capabilities by interacting with external tools and environments. Content includes OpenAI's Plug-and-Play API (where the model can decide to call a calculator, web browser, etc.), and frameworks like HuggingGPT and ToolLLM that enable an LLM to orchestrate a suite of APIs. By delegating subtasks (e.g. image analysis, database queries) to specialized tools, an AI system can overcome some limitations of a single model. Students experiment with an LLM that uses a wiki browser to answer questions ‚Äì illustrating the power of tool augmentation. We also cover memory augmentation (e.g. using vector stores as the model's long-term memory).
    Reading: Schick et al. (2023) Toolformer; Shuster et al. (2023) BlenderBot 3.
  3. Autonomous Agents and Open-Ended Task Execution: The rise of "auto" agents ‚Äì systems that can operate with minimal human intervention to achieve goals. We look at projects like AutoGPT and BabyAGI that sparked interest in looping an LLM with itself to recursively plan and act. While rudimentary, they illustrate an important concept: AI agents that set sub-goals, adjust plans, and attempt tasks continuously. We analyze an example where an AutoGPT-style agent is given a high-level goal (e.g. research a topic) and it generates a task list, executes web searches, and tries to aggregate findings. Discussion: challenges such as getting stuck in loops, error propagation, and the need for reliable self-monitoring.
    Reading: Xie et al. (2023) on Task-Driven Autonomous Agents.
  4. Multi-Agent Collaboration and Communication: Can multiple AI agents work together (or with humans) to solve problems? This week explores multi-agent systems composed of learning-based agents. We consider both homogeneous setups (multiple LLMs dialoguing or role-playing different experts) and heterogeneous ones (an LLM plus other AI modules). Students examine self-play environments where agents negotiate or collaborate. We also introduce the concept of Multi-LLM Collaborative Intelligence (MACI) ‚Äì networks of LLMs that could potentially complement each other's knowledge or cross-verify reasoning. A core question is how agents might communicate effectively in natural language to coordinate plans.
    Reading: Stanford CS372 lecture notes on multi-LLM collaboration; "Society of Minds" concept (Minsky).
  5. Generative Agents and Simulated Societies: We study the recent Stanford Generative Agents paper by Park et al. (2023), where believable simulated humans inhabit a sandbox town. These agents (driven by an LLM) wake up, make breakfast, go to work, form memories, and initiate interactions ‚Äì all without direct scripting. In class, we walk through the architecture: a memory stream for each agent, retrieval of relevant memories, reflection, and planning loops. This yields surprisingly coherent social behaviors (the famous Valentine's Day party simulation). We discuss how generative agents open new research in virtual characters, social simulations, and testing AI alignment in multi-agent settings.
    Reading: Park et al. (2023) "Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior".
  6. Lifelong Learning and Self-Adapting Systems: Most AI models are static after training, but emerging work lets models learn on the fly from new data or experiences. We cover concepts of continual learning (updating models without forgetting past knowledge) and highlight a milestone: MIT's SEAL: Self-Adapting LLMs framework. SEAL allows an LLM to generate its own training data and fine-tuning instructions to improve itself autonomously. We examine results from the SEAL paper (presented at NeurIPS 2025): the model generated "self-edits" in natural language describing how to update its weights, and using a dual-loop (inner fine-tune, outer reinforcement learning) it significantly improved performance on tasks. This suggests a path to AI systems that self-evolve their capabilities during deployment.
    Reading: Zweiger et al. (2025) "Self-Adapting LLMs (SEAL)".
  7. AI-Driven Science and Autonomous Research: Can AI not only learn by itself, but also discover new knowledge? We explore systems like Robot Scientists (e.g. Lab automation with AI) and the concept of AI Scientist 2.0. Sakana AI's AI Scientist is an example of an agent that can autonomously generate hypotheses, design experiments, and even write research papers[3]. The AI Scientist 2.0 reportedly wrote a peer-reviewed paper and improved its own research algorithms[4]. We discuss how such systems work and what advances (in reasoning, planning, and maybe robotics) enable them. This segues into the notion of a self-improving AI leading to an "intelligence explosion"[3]. Ethical and practical questions (will AI replace human scientists? how to verify AI discoveries) are debated.
    Reading: Rich Washburn (2024) on AI Scientist 2.0[4][3].
  8. Towards Artificial General Intelligence (AGI): Stepping back, we assess where current research stands on the path to AGI. We outline definitions of AGI (a system with broad, human-level cognitive abilities) and examine proposals for achieving it. Topics include cognitive architectures that combine learning with symbolic reasoning or modules (e.g. OpenAI's Coleman proposal, IBM's Safari architecture), the idea of unified world models (as seen in Genie) that could enable general learning, and discussions of emergent meta-learning (AI improving its own learning algorithms). We also consider capability evaluation frameworks ‚Äì e.g. the Levels of AGI proposed by some researchers to categorize progress. Students read and discuss a recent position paper on what's required to move from narrow AI (ASI-Level 2) to higher autonomy (ASI-Level 3/4).
    Reading: Morris et al. (2024) "Levels of AGI" (ICML).
  9. Safety and Alignment at the Frontier: As we push toward more powerful, autonomous AI, ensuring safety becomes critical. This week delves into AI Safety Level 3 and 4 topics ‚Äì the precautions and governance for advanced AI systems. We use Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy as a case study: Anthropic pre-emptively applied AI Safety Level 3 (ASL-3) protections when deploying Claude 4, involving stricter security (preventing model weight theft) and usage guardrails for high-risk knowledge (e.g. CBRN weapons). We discuss technical alignment strategies for very advanced models: red-teaming, adversarial training, interpretability tools to peer inside neural nets, and constitutional AI at scale. Students are also introduced to the concept of reward hacking and why superintelligent AI poses novel safety challenges.
    Reading: Anthropic (2025) "Activating AI Safety Level 3 Protections"; Gabriel (2023) on the alignment problem.
  10. Ethical and Societal Implications: A broader discussion on how emerging AI impacts society. We examine issues like job displacement from generative AI, misinformation via deepfakes and AI-generated content, and concentration of power (whoever controls AGI). Policy responses worldwide are covered: e.g. the EU AI Act, the US Executive Order on AI Safety, and the ACM guidelines. We debate calls for AI labs to slow down vs. the race dynamics. Special attention is given to ethical questions raised by autonomous agents ‚Äì for instance, if an AI agent misbehaves or causes harm, how do we assign responsibility? Students will consider the long-term ethical implications of AI scientist agents or AI in government.
    Reading: Future of Life Institute open letter (2023); Bryson (2019) "Robots should be slaves" (provocative take on AI rights).
  11. Student Project Presentations: Teams present the findings of their semester-long research projects on an emerging AI topic of their choice (approved earlier in the semester). Projects typically involve reproducing and extending a recent paper or conducting an experiment on course-related themes (e.g. fine-tuning an LLM for a new tool, or simulating multi-agent interactions in a game). Peers and instructors offer feedback. No new reading.
  12. Concluding Discussion ‚Äì The Road Ahead: In our final class, we synthesize lessons from both semesters and paint a picture of AI's possible future. We revisit the course's key question: What will it take to achieve robust, safe, General AI? We also address recent developments (since the start of the course ‚Äì as this field changes monthly) and forecast emerging trends for 2026 and beyond. Potential topics include: integration of neuroscience insights into AI, progress in quantum AI, or the emergence of superalignment research to align AI more complex than humans. The course ends with an open discussion, encouraging students to be the next contributors to these cutting-edge topics.
